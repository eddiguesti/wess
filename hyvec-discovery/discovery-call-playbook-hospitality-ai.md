## 1. Pre-Call Intelligence & Framing

### What You Know About Peter Edler

- Swiss-trained: values precision, structured thinking, efficiency. Will respect data-driven arguments.
- Ex-GM of Maritim Resort: understands full hotel operations from front desk to P&L.
- Currently overseeing expansion (3 new villas): under operational and capital pressure simultaneously.
- Responded positively to an email automation demo: already curious, not cold.
- Uses Opera PMS: knows its strengths and its limitations. Has likely fought with its reporting.
- Heavy Excel dependency: a sign the PMS is not serving all operational needs.
- Struggles with qualified workers: this is existential in Mauritian hospitality right now.

### Framing the Call

This is not a sales pitch. This is a peer-level operational conversation between two people who understand hotel operations. You are there to understand his world, not to present yours. The demo already did the presenting. Now you diagnose.

**Opening line (example):**

> "Peter, thank you for making the time. Before we discuss anything about what we do, I would genuinely like to understand how your operations are structured today -- particularly with the expansion underway. Would that be a reasonable starting point?"

This does three things: shows respect for his time, positions you as a listener, and establishes the call as a diagnostic conversation rather than a presentation.

## 2. SPIN Selling Framework -- Hospitality Adaptation

SPIN works by moving through four question types in sequence: Situation (gathering facts), Problem (surfacing pain), Implication (expanding the consequences), Need-payoff (letting the prospect articulate the value of solving it). The key discipline is to spend the majority of time on Implication and Need-payoff, not on Situation.

### Situation Questions (Use Sparingly -- You Already Know Much)

Purpose: Establish context. Do not over-interrogate; Peter will lose patience if you ask things you should already know. Use these to confirm and deepen, not to discover basics.

> "You mentioned the group runs on Opera. Are you on Opera Cloud or the on-premise version?"

> "For the reporting and forecasting work -- the Excel side -- roughly how many people are involved in pulling that data together each week?"

> "With three villas in development, are you planning to run them under the same Opera instance, or is that still being evaluated?"

> "How is the pre-opening team structured for the new properties? Are you building that internally or bringing in external support?"

**Key principle:** Each Situation question should earn you the right to ask a deeper Problem question. If it does not, it is wasted.

### Problem Questions (Surface the Pain)

Purpose: Get Peter to articulate what is not working. A Swiss-trained GM will not complain easily -- he will understate problems. Your job is to ask precisely enough that the truth surfaces.

> "When you pull occupancy data from Opera into Excel for your weekly management reports -- where does that process tend to break down or require the most manual correction?"

> "You mentioned the email automation caught your attention. What specifically about the current process made that resonate?"

> "With the labour situation in Mauritius as it is, are you finding it difficult to fill specific roles, or is it more a general quality issue across the board?"

> "When a guest has a complaint or a special request, how does that information move from the front desk to the relevant department today? Is there a point where things get lost?"

> "In your experience running Maritim -- what were the operational tasks that consumed the most management time for the least strategic value?"

### Implication Questions (Expand the Consequences)

Purpose: This is where the real work happens. You help Peter see that the problems he has normalised are actually costing him significantly. Implication questions connect a stated problem to broader business consequences.

> "If the weekly reporting takes -- let us say -- 15 hours of management time across the group, what does that mean over a year in terms of what those people are not doing instead?"

> "When information gets lost between front desk and housekeeping, how does that typically show up in guest satisfaction scores or online reviews?"

> "If you are opening three new properties but the labour pool is already stretched, what happens to service quality at the existing properties during that transition?"

> "When the Excel reports have errors or arrive late, how does that affect the speed of your revenue management decisions? Are you ever making pricing calls on data that is already a few days old?"

> "If a qualified reservations agent leaves tomorrow, how long does it realistically take to replace them and bring the new person to the same level of competence? What does that gap cost you in conversion rate?"

**Note on tone:** These are not rhetorical questions designed to make Peter feel bad. They are genuine inquiries. Pause after asking. Let him calculate. A Swiss-trained professional will do the maths in his head -- give him the space to arrive there himself.

### Need-Payoff Questions (Let Him Articulate the Value)

Purpose: Instead of telling Peter what your solution does, you ask questions that lead him to describe his ideal state. When he says it, he owns it. When you say it, it is a sales claim.

> "If you could get a consolidated performance dashboard that updated in real time from Opera -- without anyone touching a spreadsheet -- what would that free your management team to focus on instead?"

> "If the guest communication workflow you saw in the demo were running across all properties, how would that change the pressure on your front desk team?"

> "Suppose you could onboard a new front desk agent in two weeks instead of six, because the routine guest interactions were handled by automation. What would that mean for your expansion timeline?"

> "If you had a system that automatically flagged when a VIP guest's preferences were not being met -- before they complained -- how would that change the guest experience conversation at your properties?"

> "What would it be worth to your group if you could open the three new villas with 30% fewer front-of-house staff than you would normally plan for, without any drop in service quality?"

## 3. Gap Selling -- Current State vs. Desired State

Gap Selling (Keenan) centres on one principle: people buy to move from a problematic current state to a better future state. The size of the gap determines the urgency. Your job is to make the gap visible and quantifiable.

### Mapping the Current State

Build a clear picture across three dimensions:

**Technical current state:**
- Opera PMS (likely on-premise, limited API flexibility)
- Manual Excel reporting (occupancy, revenue, forecasting)
- Email/phone-based guest communication
- Likely minimal integration between PMS and other systems (F&B, spa, housekeeping)

**Business current state:**
- Labour shortage constraining service quality and growth
- Three properties in development (capital-intensive, management-attention-intensive)
- Manual processes consuming senior management time
- Data latency in decision-making (reports are retrospective, not real-time)

**Emotional current state:**
- Peter is a veteran -- he knows what "good" looks like. The gap between what he knows is possible and what his current setup delivers is a source of professional frustration.
- He is probably tired of solving the same operational problems he solved 15 years ago, but with fewer qualified people.

### Defining the Desired Future State

Ask Peter to describe it in his own words:

> "If we were sitting here 18 months from now and the new villas were running smoothly -- what would 'smoothly' look like to you, operationally?"

> "When you think about the kind of hotel operation you ran at Maritim at its best -- what are the two or three things that are hardest to replicate right now?"

> "If you could redesign your management reporting from scratch -- not constrained by what Opera gives you out of the box -- what would you want to see, and how often?"

### Making the Gap Explicit

Once you have both states documented, reflect them back:

> "So if I understand correctly: today your team spends roughly [X] hours per week on reporting that is still two or three days behind reality, and what you actually need is a real-time view that your GMs can check on their phones. Is that a fair summary of the gap?"

> "It sounds like the expansion is going to amplify every inefficiency you have today -- the same processes, but across more properties, with fewer people. Would you agree that is the core challenge?"

The gap becomes the business case. The larger you can make it -- together, not by exaggeration but by honest calculation -- the more obvious the need for change.

## 4. Loss Aversion -- Framing Around What Is Being Lost

### The Psychology

Kahneman and Tversky demonstrated that losses carry roughly twice the psychological weight of equivalent gains. Losing 100,000 MUR feels approximately twice as painful as gaining 100,000 MUR feels good. This means framing conversations around what is currently being lost is more motivating than framing around what could be gained.

### Application: Reframe Every Benefit as a Loss Being Stopped

Do not say: "You could save 20 hours per week with automation."
Instead frame as: "Right now, your management team is losing 20 hours every week to manual reporting -- time that cannot be recovered."

Do not say: "AI can improve your response time to guests."
Instead frame as: "Every hour a guest enquiry sits unanswered, you are losing the booking to a competitor who responded faster."

Do not say: "Automation could reduce your staffing needs."
Instead frame as: "Each month you operate the new villas without automation, you are carrying labour costs that do not need to exist."

### Specific Loss-Framed Questions for Peter

> "When you think about the hours your team spends reformatting Opera data in Excel -- what is that time actually costing you? Not just in salary, but in the decisions that get delayed or the projects that do not get attention?"

> "If a potential guest sends an enquiry and your team takes 24 hours to respond, what percentage of those bookings do you estimate you are losing to properties that respond within the hour?"

> "With the labour market as it is, every time you lose a trained staff member to a competitor, what does that cost you in recruitment, training, and the quality gap during the transition?"

> "You mentioned the new villas. Every week of delay in getting operational systems right is a week of revenue the properties are not generating. Have you calculated what that looks like?"

> "When your front desk is handling routine questions that could be automated -- confirmations, directions, check-in details -- what guest interactions are they not having? What is the cost of those missed moments to your TripAdvisor scores?"

### The Key Discipline

Never fabricate or exaggerate losses. Peter will see through it instantly. The power of loss framing is that real losses, honestly calculated, are already more motivating than gains. You do not need to inflate them.

## 5. Status Quo Bias -- Challenging "We Have Always Done It This Way"

### The Psychology

Status quo bias is the tendency to prefer the current state of affairs simply because it is familiar. It is powered by loss aversion (change might make things worse), decision fatigue (evaluating alternatives is exhausting), and regret avoidance (if I change and it fails, I will regret it more than if I stay and things remain mediocre).

### Peter's Likely Status Quo Defences

- "Opera does what we need it to do." (It does the minimum. That is not the same as what you need.)
- "We have people who know the Excel processes." (Those people could be doing higher-value work.)
- "We have been running hotels for 20 years without AI." (The labour market 20 years ago was not today's labour market.)
- "Our guests prefer the human touch." (No one is suggesting you remove it. The question is whether your humans are spending their time on the right touches.)

### Techniques to Challenge Without Confrontation

**1. The "Compared to When" Technique**

Anchor to a time when Peter's own standards were being met, then show the drift:

> "When you were running Maritim at full capacity with a strong team -- how much time did your GMs spend on operational reporting versus guest-facing leadership? How does that ratio look today?"

This lets him identify the gap himself. You are not criticising his current operation; you are letting him compare it to his own gold standard.

**2. The "If You Were Starting From Scratch" Technique**

Remove the emotional attachment to current systems:

> "If you were setting up the three new villas today with no legacy systems -- no existing Opera configuration, no established workflows -- would you design the same processes you are running now?"

The answer is almost always no. This creates cognitive dissonance with the status quo.

**3. The "Your Competitors Are Moving" Technique (Use Carefully)**

> "Some of the groups we speak with in the region are starting to automate their guest communications and reporting. I am curious -- is that something you are seeing from your competitors as well?"

This is not pressure. It is an open question that lets Peter assess his own competitive position.

**4. The "Cost of Standing Still" Technique**

> "The interesting thing about the status quo is that it is never actually static. Labour costs are rising, guest expectations are increasing, and your competitors are investing in technology. Doing nothing today is not the same as doing nothing five years ago -- it is actually falling behind. Is that something you are feeling?"

**5. The "Acknowledge the Risk of Change" Technique**

This is counterintuitive but powerful. By naming his fear, you disarm it:

> "I should be honest -- any change to operational systems carries risk, especially during an expansion phase. That is a legitimate concern. The question is whether the risk of changing is actually greater than the risk of not changing, given what is ahead of you."

## 6. Social Proof & Authority -- Referencing Industry Peers

### Principles

Social proof works because people look to peers in similar situations to validate their decisions. In B2B hospitality, the relevant peer group is: same tier of property, same region, same challenges. Authority works when the reference is a credible, respected source -- not a logo on a slide.

### How to Use It Without Name-Dropping

**1. Category Reference (Not Company Reference)**

Instead of: "The Four Seasons uses AI for guest communications."
Use: "Several luxury resort groups in the Indian Ocean region have started automating their pre-arrival guest communications. The pattern we see is a 40% reduction in front desk enquiry volume within the first three months."

This is more credible because it is a pattern, not a single anecdote.

**2. The "What We Have Observed" Frame**

> "In our work with hotel groups of a similar size -- typically 4 to 8 properties -- the ones that have moved to automated reporting from their PMS tend to reclaim between 15 and 25 hours of management time per week. The surprising thing is that the data quality also improves, because you remove the human transcription errors."

**3. The "I Am Curious If You See the Same Thing" Frame**

> "One pattern we have noticed with Opera users specifically is that the PMS captures far more data than most hotel groups actually use for decision-making. The extraction process is so manual that people only pull what they absolutely need. Is that your experience as well?"

This positions you as someone who works with Opera regularly (authority) and invites Peter to validate the observation (making him an insider to the pattern).

**4. Industry Data as Proof**

> "The hospitality industry globally is projecting a 15-20% labour shortfall in operational roles through 2028. The groups that are investing in automation now are not doing it because they want fewer people -- they are doing it because the people simply are not available."

**5. The Swiss/European Reference Point**

Peter's training is Swiss. Swiss hospitality is synonymous with precision and efficiency.

> "The Swiss approach to hotel operations -- and I say this respectfully, knowing your background -- has always been about doing more with precision rather than more with volume. What we find is that automation aligns very naturally with that philosophy. It is not about replacing the human element; it is about removing the noise so the human element can operate at the level it should."

## 7. Active Listening -- Chris Voss Techniques

These techniques come from hostage negotiation but are remarkably effective in consultative sales. The core principle is tactical empathy: demonstrating that you understand the other person's perspective, which builds trust faster than any argument.

### Technique 1: Mirroring

Repeat the last 1-3 critical words of what Peter says, with a slight upward inflection. Then pause. This encourages him to elaborate without you asking a direct question.

**Peter:** "The biggest challenge right now is that we cannot find people who understand luxury service standards."

**You:** "...who understand luxury service standards?"

**Peter** (will naturally elaborate): "Yes, we hire people and spend three months training them, and then they leave for a hotel in Dubai that pays 40% more. It is a constant drain."

You have now learned something far more specific than a direct question would have produced.

### Technique 2: Labelling Emotions

Use the phrases "It seems like..." or "It sounds like..." to name the emotion or concern behind what Peter is saying. This validates his experience and deepens the conversation.

**Peter:** "We have looked at various technology solutions before. They all promise the world and then the implementation is a nightmare."

**You:** "It sounds like there is a real frustration with vendors who underestimate the complexity of hotel operations."

**Peter:** "Exactly. They do not understand that we cannot just shut down for two weeks to implement a new system. We have guests arriving every day."

Now he has told you his implementation fear -- which you can address later.

### Technique 3: Accusation Audit

Preemptively name the negative assumptions Peter might have about you. This disarms scepticism.

At the start of the call:

> "Peter, before we begin -- you have probably spoken with technology vendors before who do not fully grasp how a hotel operation works, and I would not blame you for being cautious about whether this conversation will be any different. That is a completely reasonable position."

This accomplishes two things: it shows self-awareness, and it forces Peter to either agree (which opens the door to you differentiating yourself) or disagree (which means he is already giving you more credit than the average vendor).

### Technique 4: Calibrated Questions

Open-ended questions beginning with "How" or "What" that put Peter in the position of problem-solving alongside you:

> "What would need to be true for you to feel confident that an automation project would not disrupt your current operations?"

> "How would you measure success if you did move forward with automating your guest communications?"

> "What is the biggest obstacle you see between where you are now and where you would like to be with your operational efficiency?"

These questions give Peter agency. He is not being sold to; he is co-designing the solution.

### Technique 5: The Pause

After asking a significant question or making a labelling statement, be silent for at least 4-5 seconds. Resist the urge to fill the gap. Peter is a thoughtful professional -- he needs processing time. The most important information often comes in the seconds after the initial response.

### Technique 6: Summarising and "That's Right"

The goal of active listening is to reach the moment when Peter says "That's right" (not "You're right" -- which is dismissive, but "That's right" -- which means you have accurately captured his world).

> "So if I can summarise: you are expanding at a point when the labour market is the tightest it has been, your existing systems require significant manual work to produce the reporting you need, and the guest communication workflow is adding pressure to a front desk team that is already stretched. Meanwhile, you know what excellent operations look like from your Maritim experience, and there is a gap between that standard and what the current setup allows. Is that a fair picture?"

If he says "That is exactly right" -- you have earned the right to discuss solutions.

## 8. Pain Amplification -- The Cost of Inaction

### The Principle

Pain amplification is not about making someone feel bad. It is about making the true cost of the status quo visible. Most executives have normalised their operational pain -- they have lived with it so long that they no longer register it as a cost. Your job is to re-surface it with honest maths.

### The Time Cost Calculation

> "Let us do a rough calculation together, if you are open to it. If your team spends -- conservatively -- 15 hours per week on manual reporting across the group, that is roughly 780 hours per year. At the fully loaded cost of the people doing that work, what does that come to? And that is before we consider the opportunity cost -- what those 780 hours could have produced if spent on revenue management, guest experience, or pre-opening planning."

### The Revenue Leakage Calculation

> "What is your average booking value? And what is your current enquiry-to-booking conversion rate? If you are responding to enquiries in 12 hours instead of 1 hour, industry data suggests you may be losing 30-50% of those prospects to faster-responding competitors. On a base of [X] enquiries per month, that could represent [Y] in lost revenue. Shall we work through the numbers?"

### The Expansion Risk Amplification

> "You are building three new properties. Each one needs trained staff, working systems, and operational processes from day one. If you are replicating the same manual workflows you have now -- but across more properties, with a thinner talent pool -- the pain does not grow linearly. It compounds. Every inefficiency you tolerate in one property becomes three inefficiencies across the group."

### The Staff Turnover Amplification

> "You mentioned difficulty finding qualified workers. What does it cost you -- in recruitment fees, training time, and lost productivity -- each time you lose a front desk agent or reservations manager? If that number is [X], and you are losing [Y] people per year, the annual cost of turnover is [Z]. What if you could reduce the dependency on those hard-to-find roles by automating the routine elements of their work?"

### The "Three Years From Now" Frame

> "If nothing changes in your operations between now and when the new villas open -- same systems, same processes, same staffing model -- what does that look like three years from now? Is it sustainable?"

This question works because Peter already knows the answer. You are not telling him; you are asking him to confront what he already suspects.

## 9. Commitment & Consistency -- Small Yeses

### The Principle

Cialdini's commitment and consistency principle states that once people take a small position or agree to a small request, they are psychologically inclined to remain consistent with that position. In discovery calls, this means building a chain of agreements that lead naturally toward engagement.

### The Micro-Agreement Chain

**Agreement 1 -- The Problem Exists:**

> "Would you agree that the manual reporting process is consuming more management time than it should?"

(Easy yes. He already knows this.)

**Agreement 2 -- The Problem Has a Cost:**

> "And that time has a real cost -- both in salary and in what those people could be doing instead?"

(Logical extension. Hard to disagree.)

**Agreement 3 -- The Problem Will Get Worse:**

> "With three new properties coming online, that workload is going to increase rather than decrease, correct?"

(He is already thinking about this.)

**Agreement 4 -- A Solution Would Be Valuable:**

> "So a system that could eliminate most of that manual work -- and give you better data in real time -- that would be worth exploring seriously?"

(He has already agreed to the premises. This is the consistent conclusion.)

**Agreement 5 -- A Next Step Makes Sense:**

> "Would it be useful if we mapped out exactly which of your current workflows are candidates for automation, so you could see the potential scope before making any decisions?"

This is not a close. It is an offer to do more diagnostic work -- which is consistent with everything he has agreed to so far.

### Other Micro-Commitments

- "Would it be helpful if I sent you a brief summary of what we discussed today?" (Gets him to engage with a follow-up document.)
- "Would you be open to including your IT lead or operations director in the next conversation?" (Expands the stakeholder group -- a buying signal.)
- "If I put together a rough impact analysis based on the numbers we discussed, would that be worth reviewing?" (He agrees to evaluate data -- a step toward a proposal.)

### Key Rule

Never ask for a commitment that feels like a leap. Each step should feel like a natural, low-risk extension of what has already been agreed. The moment Peter feels manoeuvred, you lose the trust of a Swiss-trained professional.

## 10. Cultural Considerations -- Mauritius Business Norms

### The Cultural Landscape

Mauritius sits at the intersection of French, British, Indian, and Creole influences. Business culture leans Franco-Mauritian in the hospitality sector, particularly in the luxury segment. Peter, being Swiss-trained and having worked at senior levels in Mauritius, operates in a professional culture that is:

- **Relationship-first:** Business decisions in Mauritius are made between people who trust each other, not between companies. The relationship precedes the transaction.
- **Hierarchical but accessible:** Seniority is respected, but senior leaders in hospitality are often hands-on. Peter is likely both strategic and operational.
- **Indirect on disagreement:** Mauritians (and the Franco-Mauritian business class especially) tend to avoid direct confrontation. A "maybe" often means "no." Silence after a question may mean discomfort, not agreement.
- **Long-term oriented:** Mauritian business relationships are built over multiple interactions. Expecting a decision in one call is culturally tone-deaf.
- **French-influenced formality:** Use "Monsieur Edler" until invited to use first names. Written follow-ups should be polished. Avoid casual language.

### What to Avoid (The "Not American" List)

| Do Not | Do Instead |
|--------|-----------|
| "This is awesome!" | "This is quite promising." |
| "Let's crush it!" | "Let us see how we can make this work." |
| "I'm super excited about this!" | "I find this genuinely interesting." |
| "What's keeping you up at night?" | "What are the operational priorities weighing on you at the moment?" |
| Hard close / urgency pressure | "There is no rush on a decision. Let us make sure the thinking is sound." |
| Fake scarcity ("We only have 2 spots left") | Never. Peter will see through it and lose respect for you. |
| Over-familiarity on first call | Professional warmth, not buddy energy. |
| Interrupting his answers | Let him finish. Then pause. Then respond. |

### Positive Cultural Signals

- **Reference his experience respectfully:** "With your background in running a property of Maritim's scale..." -- this honours his career without flattery.
- **Acknowledge the Mauritian hospitality tradition:** "The service standards in Mauritius are genuinely world-class -- the challenge seems to be sustaining them with a changing workforce."
- **Use measured language:** "We have observed..." rather than "We totally see this everywhere!" Understatement is more credible than enthusiasm.
- **Show patience with the process:** "I appreciate that a decision like this needs proper evaluation. Shall we set up a time to continue the conversation when you have had a chance to reflect?"
- **Demonstrate knowledge of the island's challenges:** Labour migration to the Gulf, seasonal tourism patterns, the cost of importing skilled workers -- showing you understand these signals that you have done your homework.

### Peter-Specific Considerations

Peter is Swiss-trained. This means:

- **Punctuality is non-negotiable.** Start on time. End on time. If you go over, ask permission.
- **Precision matters.** Vague promises will destroy credibility. If you do not know something, say so. "I would need to verify that" is far better than a guess.
- **He will evaluate ROI rigorously.** Be prepared to discuss numbers. Not vague percentages -- actual calculations based on his data.
- **He values competence over charisma.** Do not try to charm him. Know your material. Answer questions directly. Admit limitations.
- **He respects structured thinking.** If you send a follow-up, it should be well-organised, concise, and actionable. Not a long narrative. A clear document with sections, numbers, and next steps.

## 11. Call Structure & Sequence

### Recommended 45-Minute Structure

**Minutes 0-3: Opening and Framing**
- Thank him for his time.
- Accusation audit: "You have probably spoken to technology vendors who do not understand hotel operations..."
- State the purpose: "I would like to understand your operations before discussing anything about what we might offer."
- Ask permission to ask questions.

**Minutes 3-12: Situation & Context (Light Touch)**
- Confirm Opera setup, property count, team structure.
- Understand the expansion timeline.
- Clarify what prompted interest after the email automation demo.
- Mirror and label throughout.

**Minutes 12-25: Problem Discovery & Implication**
- This is the core of the call.
- Use SPIN Problem and Implication questions.
- Use Gap Selling to map current state vs. desired state.
- Apply loss aversion framing where natural.
- Challenge status quo gently where he normalises pain.
- Listen more than you speak. Target a 70/30 ratio (him/you).

**Minutes 25-35: Need-Payoff & Future State**
- Ask what "good" looks like to him.
- Let him describe the ideal operation.
- Reflect the gap back to him.
- Use the "Three years from now" frame.
- Seek "That's right" moment with a comprehensive summary.

**Minutes 35-42: Micro-Commitments & Next Steps**
- "Would it be useful if we..." (offer a concrete, low-risk next step).
- Suggest a scope-mapping exercise or workflow audit.
- Ask if there are other stakeholders who should be involved.
- Agree on a specific follow-up date.

**Minutes 42-45: Close with Respect**
- Thank him for the depth of the conversation.
- Summarise the two or three key points.
- Confirm the next step and timeline.
- "I will send you a summary by [date]. If anything else comes to mind in the meantime, please do not hesitate to reach out."

24. "Is doing nothing today actually the same as doing nothing five years ago, given how the market has shifted?"

### Social Proof
25. "Several luxury resort groups in the Indian Ocean region have started automating pre-arrival communications. Are you seeing that from competitors?"
26. "Opera users we work with typically find they are using about 30% of the data the PMS captures. Is that your experience?"
27. "The hospitality industry is projecting a 15-20% labour shortfall through 2028."

### Active Listening (Voss)
28. Mirror: "...luxury service standards?" (repeat his last key phrase)
29. Label: "It sounds like there is real frustration with vendors who underestimate hotel complexity."
30. Calibrated: "What would need to be true for you to feel confident this would not disrupt operations?"

### Pain Amplification
31. "Let us do a rough calculation together -- 15 hours per week times 52 weeks is 780 hours per year. At what cost?"
32. "If turnover costs you [X] per departure and you lose [Y] people per year, the annual cost is [Z]."
33. "If nothing changes between now and when the villas open, is that sustainable?"

### Commitment & Consistency
34. "Would you agree the manual reporting is consuming more management time than it should?"
35. "And that time has a real cost -- both direct and opportunity cost?"
36. "So a system that eliminated most of that manual work would be worth exploring seriously?"
37. "Would it be useful if we mapped which workflows are candidates for automation?"

## Final Notes on Tone and Delivery

- Speak at a measured pace. No rushing.
- Use complete sentences. No filler words.
- "I think" is weaker than "What we have observed is..."
- Never oversell. Undersell and overdeliver.
- If you do not know something, say: "That is a good question. I would need to look into that and come back to you with a precise answer."
- Treat Peter as a peer, not a prospect. He has more hospitality experience than you. Respect that.
- The goal of this call is not to close. The goal is to earn the next conversation.
